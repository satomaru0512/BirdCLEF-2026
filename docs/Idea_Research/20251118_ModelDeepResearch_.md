UAV RGB-Based Pasture Biomass Estimation: Models, Backbones, and Techniques Figure: UAV image-based pasture biomass estimation pipeline. UAVs capture top-down images of pasture quadrats, alongside on-site NDVI and height measurements; laboratory processing provides ground-truth dry biomass for green grass, clover, and dead matter. AI models learn to predict total and component biomass (green, clover, dead) from these inputs (Image: CSIRO Kaggle Pasture Biomass Challenge infographic). 1. Deep Learning Research on UAV RGB for Biomass (AGB) Prediction Early studies showed that UAV-derived spectral vs. structural features each provide partial insight. Using only color/vegetation indices from RGB tends to yield modest accuracy (R² ~0.45–0.72), while using only height/3D structure (DSM, DTM from SfM) gives R² ~0.54–0.81 mdpi.com . Combining both significantly improves performance. For example, Schaefer & Lamb (2016) found that integrating NDVI measurements with LiDAR-derived canopy height improved pasture biomass estimation in tall fescue arxiv.org . A recent study by Vahidi et al. (2023) extracted canopy height models (CHM) from UAV photogrammetry alongside RGB color statistics to train an ANN model (MLP). This yielded high precision (R² ≈ 0.94, RMSE ~62 g/m²) in estimating paddock biomass mdpi.com mdpi.com , outperforming prior CNN attempts (AlexNet/ResNet reached R² ≈ 0.88) mdpi.com . Their results underscore that ultra-high-resolution CHM + RGB features capture complementary information (plant height + greenness) critical for biomass prediction mdpi.com mdpi.com . Another key factor is camera perspective and multi-view imagery. Most UAV studies use nadir (top-down) images, but Sun et al. (2024) demonstrated that adding oblique-angle images substantially improves AGB estimates across diverse grasslands researchgate.net researchgate.net . They collected images at 5–50 m heights and 5 angles (30°–90°). Vegetation indices (e.g. NDI, VARI, MGRVI) computed from a 45° oblique view complemented the 90° (nadir) view, reducing error due to canopy occlusion and lighting effects researchgate.net researchgate.net . The optimal setup was 20 m altitude at 45° angle, which yielded the highest R² (up to ~0.81 in alpine meadows) and lowest RMSE researchgate.net researchgate.net . Machine learning models (RF, XGBoost, MLP) benefited more from oblique data (average R² ~0.75) than simple linear regressions (R² ~0.60) researchgate.net . This highlights that multi-angle imagery helps capture canopy volume and density, thus improving biomass predictions (especially in heterogeneous or tall swards). Deep learning models have been applied to directly regress biomass from RGB images as well. Castro et al. (2020) evaluated standard CNN architectures (AlexNet, ResNet-18) for forage biomass prediction and reported a high Pearson correlation of ~0.88 (≈ R² 0.77) with MAE ≈13% mdpi.com mdpi.com . They found that using ImageNet-pretrained weights and data augmentation improved accuracy, though very deep networks like VGG struggled without enough training data mdpi.com mdpi.com . Notably, they emphasize that future models should incorporate 3D features (point-cloud derived volume) to further boost performance, given biomass’s strong relationship with plant volume mdpi.com mdpi.com . In summary, the research consensus is that multi-modal inputs (RGB texture, spectral indices, height maps) and multi-view images yield the best biomass predictions. Approaches that fuse these (either as input features to an ANN mdpi.com or via multi-branch CNNs) consistently outperform single-source models. This motivates designs where a CNN learns from the image while additional channels (e.g. a derived CHM or vegetation index map) are fed into the network or used in post-prediction fusion, to leverage all available information about the pasture’s structure and health. 2. Modern Vision Backbones for Feature Extraction To achieve high accuracy from limited agricultural data, leveraging large pretrained vision backbones is crucial. Instead of training small networks (e.g. EfficientNet, ConvNeXt) from scratch, current best practices use foundation models pretrained on massive image corpora. One promising backbone is DINOv2 (2023), a self-supervised ViT (Vision Transformer) trained on a curated 142M-image dataset. DINOv2 produces general-purpose visual features that are robust across domains lightly.ai arxiv.org . The largest DINOv2 model (ViT-G/14, ~1B parameters) was distilled into smaller models that surpassed OpenCLIP on numerous vision benchmarks arxiv.org . These features can be directly fine-tuned for regression – for instance, a DINOv2 encoder can serve as a powerful feature extractor for pasture images, given its ability to recognize complex textures and object details. Another state-of-the-art backbone is EVA-CLIP (2023), a series of vision-language models that improve upon OpenAI’s CLIP. EVA-CLIP introduced training optimizations enabling much larger scales. Notably, the 5 billion-parameter EVA-02-CLIP model achieved 82.0% zero-shot top-1 on ImageNet, a new state-of-the-art arxiv.org . Even a 430M param variant reached 80.4% zero-shot accuracy arxiv.org , reflecting extremely strong visual representations. Using EVA-CLIP’s vision encoder (a ViT trained on image-text pairs) as the backbone for a biomass regression model could bring both high-level semantic understanding and low-level feature sensitivity. Similarly, Google’s SigLIP2 (2025) is a cutting-edge multilingual vision-language encoder that has shown excellent transfer capability. SigLIP2’s visual backbone is a ViT-L/14 trained with multiple objectives – contrastive image-text alignment plus captioning, masked image modeling, and self-distillation emergentmind.com emergentmind.com . This multi-task pretraining yields features with improved semantic alignment and spatial localization emergentmind.com emergentmind.com . In practice, a SigLIP2 visual encoder fine-tuned on pasture images can excel at distinguishing fine-grained vegetation attributes (it was demonstrated to outperform traditional CLIP/DINO encoders on multimodal reasoning tasks) emergentmind.com emergentmind.com . Overall, these large vision backbones (DINOv2, EVA-CLIP, SigLIP2, etc.) provide a strong starting point for biomass estimation. They encode rich information about color, texture, and objects, learned from billions of images, which helps generalize to the varied appearances of pastures. A common approach is to take a pretrained ViT, attach a lightweight regression head (e.g. MLP layers), and fine-tune on the pasture dataset. This way, even with a limited dataset (~1k images in the CSIRO challenge), the model can leverage prior visual knowledge. In addition, vision-language models’ encoders can implicitly understand contextual cues (like what healthy vs. senescent grass looks like) that might correlate with biomass. Using these backbones is preferred over smaller architectures – indeed, initial Kaggle community explorations have moved away from older CNNs toward transformers and ensemble of foundation models for higher accuracy. 3. Remote Sensing & Agriculture-Specific Models In parallel to generic vision models, the remote sensing community has developed specialized models and datasets for biomass and vegetation analysis. The CSIRO Image2Biomass dataset itself is novel in its scope – it provides 1,162 high-resolution pasture images across 19 sites and all seasons, with detailed ground truth (dry biomass split into green, clover, dead fractions) arxiv.org arxiv.org . This builds on earlier datasets like GrassClover (Denmark, 435 images) but adds more diversity (multiple species, locations, year-round sampling) and extra sensor data (paired NDVI and compressed height measurements) arxiv.org arxiv.org . Such multimodal data invites models that go beyond RGB. For example, one could feed the NDVI map or height as additional input channels to a CNN. The challenge description explicitly encourages using the provided NDVI and other public data to enhance models. A straightforward method is early-fusion: stack the RGB and NDVI as a 4-channel image so that a convolutional backbone learns from both visual texture and the NDVI signal. Alternatively, one could train separate sub-networks (one processing the image, another ingesting scalar features like average NDVI or field metadata) and then combine their features in a fusion layer. Past studies in precision agriculture have found success in such multi-modal networks. For instance, Diao et al. (2020) estimated winter wheat biomass by combining UAV RGB images with the 3D point cloud-derived canopy height; a dual-branch CNN learned texture features and height map features jointly, improving biomass R² compared to RGB alone mdpi.com mdpi.com . We expect similar multi-modal fusion to be beneficial here – e.g. using the provided Height_Ave_cm and NDVI values as inputs alongside images could boost the model’s ability to handle cases where color alone is ambiguous (such as tall dry grass vs short green regrowth). The CSIRO news release also notes that their dataset allows models to learn “in more than one way,” either directly from imagery or by combining imagery with plant health indices to improve accuracy csiro.au . Beyond this competition, there are agriculture-specific vision models emerging. The Agriculture-Vision workshop series (CVPR 2020–2024) has spurred tailored architectures for field imagery (mostly for segmentation and anomaly detection). While those are segmentation-focused, some concepts carry over – e.g. using context modules to handle field-scale variability, or domain normalization to cope with lighting differences. For biomass/yield regression, researchers have proposed models like Regressive U-Net (ReUse), which adds regression output heads to U-Net architectures to predict continuous values like carbon stock or biomass per pixel github.com . Such models, originally applied to satellite biomass mapping, could potentially be adapted to UAV scale by increasing resolution and adjusting input channels. Another example is Vibrant-ViT (2023), a vision transformer adapted for high-resolution canopy height modeling in forests plantsciencedronemethods.github.io . Vision transformers can be effective in remote sensing tasks when combined with patch-wise training or local-window attention to handle large images. For global-scale biomass, Sialelli et al. (2025) introduced AGBD (Above-Ground Biomass Dataset) github.com , a dataset curating multi-sensor satellite data (Sentinel-2 optical, Sentinel-1 SAR, DEM, land cover) to predict biomass globally github.com . They trained a multi-branch model on this data, and their best architecture (“NICO”) is made available with pre-trained weights github.com github.com . While satellite-based, such a model captures broad relationships (e.g. spectral reflectance to biomass) and could possibly inform UAV-scale models through transfer learning or at least serve as a benchmark. It’s also worth noting that multi-temporal approaches are common in crop biomass estimation (e.g. using time-series of imagery to estimate growth). In this pasture dataset, we primarily have single snapshots per quadrat, but future work could integrate time-lapse data if available or apply recurrent models if sequences were provided. For now, models must rely on single-image inference, making the above-discussed strong backbones and multi-modal data integration crucial for capturing all relevant signals (color, structure, context). Domain-specific data augmentations can be applied too: e.g. simulating varied sun illumination, or using cutout/mixup to mimic patchy bare soil, as these might improve robustness in an outdoor imaging context mdpi.com mdpi.com . In summary, leveraging remote sensing-tailored models (which handle multi-band inputs, high resolution, and aerial perspective) and datasets (for pretraining or additional training data) can complement generic models. The combination of domain knowledge (e.g. vegetation indices, known agronomic relationships) with deep models is a key theme in state-of-the-art solutions. 4. Multi-Task Regression and Physically Constrained Losses A unique aspect of this challenge is the compositional target structure: Dry_Green + Dry_Clover + Dry_Dead = Dry_Total (and Dry_Green + Dry_Clover = GDM, the green dry matter). Traditional single-output regression ignores these relationships, but treating it as a multi-task learning problem allows the model to predict all components jointly and potentially enforce consistency. A common architecture is to use a shared backbone (CNN or transformer) that feeds into multiple heads – one for each output. This way, the network learns a shared representation of the image and then fine-tunes it to each biomass component, which often improves overall performance due to shared features (e.g. recognizing lush green areas helps predict both green biomass and total) arxiv.org . However, without special handling, a naive multi-output model might produce predictions that violate the known sums (for example, predicting a total that is lower than the sum of its parts). To address this, researchers and practitioners implement physical constraint losses or architectural constraints. One approach is to explicitly enforce the additive constraint in the model’s structure: for instance, have the network output only three components (Green, Dead, Clover) and then compute the total as the sum of those outputs. This guarantees consistency by construction. In fact, at least one Kaggle solution has adopted this strategy – predicting only Dry_Total, GDM, and Dry_Green with the other two components computed by subtraction (Dry_Clover = GDM – Green, Dry_Dead = Total – GDM) kaggle.com . By doing so, the model focuses on the core degrees of freedom (three independent variables instead of five) and inherently satisfies Dry_Total = Dry_Green + Dry_Clover + Dry_Dead. Participants report that this can simplify learning and avoid physically impossible predictions. Another approach is to include a consistency term in the loss function. For example, if the model outputs ${\hat{G}, \hat{C}, \hat{D}, \hat{T}}$ for green, clover, dead, total, one can add a penalty $L_{\text{cons}} = MSE(\hat{T}, \hat{G}+\hat{C}+\hat{D})$ (and similarly $MSE(\hat{\text{GDM}}, \hat{G}+\hat{C})$ if GDM is an output). The total training loss would be $L = \sum_i MSE(\hat{y}i, y_i) + \lambda L{\text{cons}}$ for some weight $\lambda$. This encourages the network to adjust its outputs to satisfy the sums. Such compositional loss ideas are analogous to consistency regularization used in other domains. For instance, Seo et al. (2021) introduced multi-output consistency in a tumor segmentation network by coupling outputs via residual connections, which improved performance pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov . In our case, while not published in literature yet for biomass, the principle holds: enforcing known relationships can act as a regularizer. It can also slightly reduce the effective search space for the model, making training easier. Care is needed in weighting the consistency loss so as not to overshadow the main prediction losses – typically one might use a relatively high $\lambda$ since physical laws should hold strictly, but if the ground truth has measurement noise, a softer enforcement might work better. Finally, a note on evaluation vs. training: the competition’s weighted R² metric heavily emphasizes Dry_Total (50% weight). Thus, models should prioritize accuracy in total biomass. A multi-task loss could also be weighted (e.g. give 5× weight to total’s error during training to reflect its importance). Many multi-output regression frameworks use equal weighting by default, but here a weighted loss (mirroring evaluation weights) might yield better scores. Alternatively, some teams perform a post-processing adjustment: e.g. ensuring non-negativity of predictions and adjusting any minor sum discrepancies by redistribution. Ensuring non-negative outputs can be done by using a final activation (like ReLU or exponential) on each output; however, most top solutions simply clamp negatives to zero post hoc if they occur, since severe negative predictions are rare with proper training. In essence, multi-task learning with output coupling is a powerful approach for this problem – it leverages the fact that the outputs are not independent. By incorporating domain knowledge (mass balance of components) into the model, we not only improve realism of predictions but often see better generalization. This has been reflected in challenge discussions, where enforcing sum constraints or dimension reduction techniques (e.g. predicting principal components of the target vector and then reconstructing to enforce correlation) have been proposed kaggle.com . As multi-output regression becomes more common in agronomy (predicting yields of multiple crop components, etc.), we expect to see more such physics-informed deep learning in the literature. 5. Open-Source Models and Codebases (PyTorch) There are several notable open-source projects relevant to UAV-based biomass and canopy modeling, which can serve as references or starting points: HighResCanopyHeight (Meta AI, 2023) – This is a PyTorch codebase for predicting canopy height maps from aerial RGB images github.com . It accompanies a paper where a self-supervised ViT encoder (pretrained on satellite imagery) is combined with a convolutional decoder and trained using aerial LiDAR data as ground truth github.com . The model produces high-resolution per-pixel height estimates. While targeted at forest canopy height, the approach is transferable: one could adapt it to predict a pasture’s biomass map (or average biomass) from the image. The use of a huge pretrained encoder and training on real 3D data allowed the model to generalize across regions, demonstrating the power of large-scale pretraining for canopy-related predictions github.com github.com . The repository provides pretrained weights and inference code that might be repurposed for biomass (e.g. by retraining the decoder on biomass values). Depth Any Canopy (Rege Cambrin et al., 2024) – This is an ECCV2024 workshop contribution that fine-tuned a foundation depth estimation model (Depth-Anything v2) for canopy height estimation github.com . The authors show that a relatively lightweight model (97M params) adapted from a generic depth model can surpass state-of-the-art in canopy height prediction with minimal training cost github.com . They release small (25M) and base (97M) models via HuggingFace, usable with the Transformers library’s AutoModelForDepthEstimation github.com github.com . This is relevant in that depth estimation models are trained to infer geometric structure from images; biomass is closely related to structure (plant volume). A creative idea is to pipeline such a model: first use a depth-estimation model on the pasture image to predict a pseudo-height map or depth features, then feed those features into a biomass regression model. Or directly fine-tune the depth model on biomass labels (as they did for canopy height). The code (PyTorch Lightning based) is publicly available and demonstrates efficient transfer learning from a vision foundation model to an environmental variable prediction task. BioMassters & AGBD repositories – The BioMassters was a DrivenData challenge (2022) to predict forest above-ground biomass from Sentinel-1 (SAR) and Sentinel-2 (optical) time-series. Baseline code (PyTorch Lightning) using a 3D U-Net on image time-stacks is available github.com . Also, top solutions (1st place by kbrodt, etc.) have been open-sourced, showcasing techniques like temporal ensembling and transformer encoders for multi-date satellite data github.com . For our UAV context, the temporal aspect is less, but these solutions illustrate advanced fusion of multispectral and radar data, heavy augmentation, and ensembling, which are instructive. Similarly, the AGBD GitHub (2025) provides the inference pipeline for the global biomass model by Sialelli et al. github.com . Their best model ingests multi-layer raster data (Sentinel reflectances, radar backscatter, DEM, land cover) and outputs biomass; it is built with a modular PyTorch framework and includes pretrained weights github.com github.com . While geared to coarse-resolution data, the modular design could be useful to plug in UAV data as “another sensor” in a multi-sensor model. It’s also a demonstration of deploying a biomass model with modern ML ops (packaging in Notebooks, using GEE for data prep, etc.) github.com github.com , which could inspire how to handle data pipeline for UAV imagery at scale. Regressive UNet (ReUse) – This model was proposed for estimating carbon stocks/biomass from remote sensing. The concept is a U-Net that directly outputs a continuous map of biomass (instead of class segmentation) github.com . The original implementation was in TensorFlow, but open re-implementations in PyTorch exist. It’s essentially a standard U-Net encoder-decoder, but trained with a regression loss (e.g. L1/L2) to predict biomass per pixel or per region. For our task (predicting a single value per image), one could still use a U-Net to output a low-resolution biomass map and then average it, or simply use the encoder part with a global pooling. The key takeaway from ReUse is the idea of repurposing segmentation networks for regression by removing the final softmax and using linear activations, which has worked well in some deforestation and biomass mapping studies github.com . GitHub code for point-cloud/Digital Twin approaches – Some projects estimate biomass by first reconstructing 3D point clouds from UAV images (via Structure-from-Motion) and then using deep learning on the 3D data. For example, Kalimantan Forest Height & Biomass (2021) is a repository that uses LiDAR and PlanetScope satellite data to estimate tropical forest biomass with deep learning github.com . And Harry Seely’s Biomass-DL repo uses point cloud deep networks (PyTorch PointNet++ etc.) on LiDAR data for tree biomass github.com . While these are beyond pure RGB imagery, they hint at how height info can be incorporated. In our context, if one had a dense point cloud from multiple UAV images, a model could directly predict biomass from that (treating it as a 3D input). However, the competition likely expects solutions focused on the RGB and tabular data provided, so these are more exploratory avenues. In summary, there is a rich ecosystem of open-source tools to draw on. Participants can expedite development by fine-tuning existing pretrained models (e.g. ViTs from DINOv2 or EVA-CLIP, depth models, etc.) rather than coding everything from scratch. They can also reference prior implementations for ideas on architecture and loss functions: for instance, Azavea’s biomass-estimation code demonstrates how to combine Sentinel-1 and -2 in a PyTorch model github.com , which is analogous to combining RGB and NDVI here. The Meta HighResCanopyHeight code shows how to effectively utilize a huge transformer backbone for a regression map task github.com . By studying these, one can assemble a state-of-the-art solution for UAV biomass estimation that employs a strong vision backbone, integrates multi-modal data, and respects the physical consistency of outputs. The open-source models illustrate that many components (backbone, decoder, training tricks) are readily available – it’s a matter of selecting and tuning them for this pasture biomass domain. With the competition running until Jan 2026, we can expect that winning solutions will indeed leverage these advanced techniques: a fusion of powerful pretrained vision models with domain-specific enhancements, multi-task learning for component outputs, and rigorous use of the rich dataset (images + NDVI + height). Each of the research directions discussed (multi-view imaging, multi-modal input, large-scale pretraining, and consistency constraints) is likely to contribute to pushing the biomass prediction accuracy closer to the practical limits, ultimately providing farmers and researchers with reliable AI tools for grazing management csiro.au csiro.au .
